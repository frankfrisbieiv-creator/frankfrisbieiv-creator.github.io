The studies
Executive power · 1953–2025

Presidents govern by decree when they lose control of Congress.

Testing ~3,900 executive orders against party control of Congress, divided government shows no reliable effect on how many orders a president issues once each president is compared against himself. But the null is fragile: it turns on how three transition years are coded, and an independent coding disagrees with ours on two of them, so neither the effect nor its absence is robust to coding choices. An initial data pull silently missed a third of the orders and had faked a result, and a completeness check caught it before publication.

Legislative organization · 1993–2022

The committees that write farm, defense, and land policy are stacked with ideological outliers.

Across fifteen Congresses, the committees that write farm, defense, and land policy sit almost exactly on their chamber's ideological median, even under a deliberately sharper test that holds each committee's party ratio fixed. The pre-registered "preference outlier" hypothesis isn't supported. But that surface centrism hides something: within each party, the committees pull in opposite directions: Agriculture and Armed Services Democrats sort more conservative, Appropriations Republicans more liberal, and the two cancel at the median. A replication of Krehbiel (1990), stress-tested against its own weakest assumption.

Campaign finance, 2010 to 2024

Has super PAC money gone dark over the last decade?

The popular story is that outside money keeps getting harder to trace. Following every super PAC dollar back through committee transfers to its original source, across eight federal cycles, it did not. The share tracing to a named individual held steady near 55 percent (dollar-weighted) and the share dead-ending at a non-disclosing organization near 26 percent, with no reliable trend in either. What the data does show is a gap the averages hide: the largest super PACs are the most individual-funded and the most traceable, while opaque, committee-routed money is spread across many smaller ones. Big outside money is mostly a handful of named megadonors, not anonymous machinery. Every figure is a floor on opacity in the disclosed record, not a measure of total dark money.

The method

The same discipline runs through every study. It is the point of the portfolio, more than any single result.

Freeze first. The hypothesis and the full analysis plan are written and time-stamped before any data is seen, so a result can't be quietly reverse-engineered into a story.

Absence isn't evidence. Missing data is treated as ignorance, never as a result. Pipelines validate their own completeness and halt rather than analyze a silent gap. That discipline has already caught a result that was faked by incomplete data.

Report the null. "The popular claim doesn't hold" is a finding, published as plainly as any positive one, with the limitations stated up front rather than buried.

Show the work. Every figure traces to a public source; the code and data are public, and a stranger can clone the repository and re-run the entire analysis.

Source for every study is public on GitHub.

Built in R + Quarto. Methodology derived from a systematic-research framework called ARIA.
